Significance

The philosophers of Ancient Greece tended to think of the world in terms of its most fundamental truths, which can be used to derive further truths. In contrast, the world today often attempts to find deeper truths by observing higher-level, more abstract concepts. For example, a Greek would think about how x affects f(x), while a modern physicist might graph f(x) to find how it is affected by changes in x.

The point is not to prove one approach or the other better: they both have their merits and are advantageous to use in different situations. If a physicist attempted to think about classical mechanics in terms of the individual subatomic particles and their interactions, they would never get anywhere. We need to generate abstractions to help us think at larger scales. Likewise, some problems require a more theoretical approach to get exact results. Calculus in particular lends itself well to this approach, as it allows the user to deal with infinitesimally small changes.

In conclusion, physics is the fine art of approximation. As physicists, we try to find exact solutions when possible but also appreciate the value of "good enough." Oftentimes, an approximation will be used instead of the more precise relationship because the values are not significantly different under the given conditions. Maintaining strict exactitude would simply increase the difficulty of the problem unnecessarily, and even make it more likely for the calculation to go wrong.
