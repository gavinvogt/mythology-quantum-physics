Conclusions

Democritus

Democritus is famous for proposing that the atom was the indivisible particle. This represents the discretization of matter, since it consists of finite steps.

Aristotle

Aristotle's book Metaphysics discussed sources of change in an object's potentiality. He concluded that "potentiality and actuality are different," which can be interpreted to mean that what an object is doing differs from what it could be doing. This relates to superposition since there is a collection of potential states, but the particle must "choose" one when measured.

Parmenides and Heraclitus

Parmenides and Heraclitus struggled with the idea of whether "beliefs accurately represent reality." Does what we think a particle is doing match with what it is really doing? Once again, this philosophical deliberation provides a link between the ancient Greeks and modern physics. Beliefs about a particle's state cannot be sure which of its possible states it is truly in, until it has been measured. This could also loosely connect to the uncertainty principle, which is a fundamental limit to how precisely we can simultaneously know two pieces of information about a particle. In these cases, there is no possible belief that can "accurately represent reality," aside from the belief that we do not accurately know reality.

Heraclitus is commonly known as the father of process philosophy, which holds that nature is "ongoingly and inexorably characterized" by process. From a quantum standpoint, this seems like a reasonable conclusion since since state alone cannot perfectly define all particles, and by extension, nature.

Zeno was a philosopher who studied under Heraclitus and proved quantization of distance by contradiction. He argued that if distance could be divided into an infinite number of steps, it would be impossible to run past a tortoise since you always need to first reach the position where the tortoise just was.
